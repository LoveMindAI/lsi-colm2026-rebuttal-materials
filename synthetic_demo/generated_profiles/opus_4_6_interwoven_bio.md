# opus_4_6_interwoven_bio

# Megyn Velasquez-Greenblum

The kitchen shelves in the Greenblum house on Tennyson Street hold a row of vintage Pyrex bowls—butterprint turquoise, spring blossom green, a faded golden wheat pattern with a hairline crack along the rim. Megyn keeps them where the morning light catches them, not behind glass, because her grandmother never believed dishes were meant to be locked away. She uses the largest one every Passover for her mother-in-law's charoset recipe, and every Día de los Muertos for her own mother's arroz con leche. The crack in the wheat bowl worries her sometimes, a low hum of anxiety about losing something irreplaceable, but she uses it anyway. That's the kind of calculation she makes constantly—risk weighed against the warmth of keeping things alive and in circulation.

She was born in Albuquerque in March of 1987, the daughter of Elena Velasquez, a school librarian, and Howard Greenblum (no relation to her future husband, a coincidence that made their wedding program a typographical headache), a CPA who ran a small firm near Old Town. Growing up between two cultures gave her a particular attentiveness to fairness—who gets included, who gets overlooked, whose name gets mispronounced and how many times before someone corrects the record. She learned early that she would rather be the one who corrects it gently than the one who lets it slide.

At Colorado State she found environmental engineering the way some people find religion: through a single revelatory moment. A hydrology professor took the class to a crumbling acequia outside Fort Collins and explained how gravity, community governance, and three centuries of maintenance could move water across a desert without a single electric pump. Megyn graduated with honors in 2009, and the organizing principle of her professional life was already set—water belongs to everyone, and the systems that deliver it should be built with that in mind.

She has worked at Meridian Water Consultants in Denver since 2012, where she is known for project files so meticulously organized that new hires are handed her templates on day one. Deadlines do not rattle her; she meets them through preparation rather than heroics. Her desk holds a laminated checklist for every active project, updated in red pen each morning before she opens email. Colleagues sometimes joke that Megyn could plan a moon landing on a spreadsheet and still come in under budget. She laughs at this, though not loudly—she is not the person who fills a conference room with her voice. She contributes steadily, listens more than she speaks in large meetings, and saves her sharper observations for smaller conversations where she feels the ground beneath her.

In evaluative settings—presentations to clients, public comment hearings—a flicker of self-consciousness surfaces, a tightness in her sternum she has learned to breathe through rather than avoid. She is not someone who craves the spotlight, but she will step into it when the work demands it, particularly when the work involves communities that have been underserved. On Saturday mornings she volunteers with Raíces de Agua, a nonprofit that installs rain gardens in neighborhoods across the Denver metro where stormwater infrastructure has been neglected for decades. She shows up with her own spade and a patience for slow progress that the organization's founder once described as "almost geological."

Her marriage to David Greenblum—her college friend, now a middle school history teacher—began in October 2015 under a canopy of aspens near Durango, with fewer than forty guests and a chuppah draped in her grandmother's rebozos. Their household runs on a quiet cooperative logic: shared calendars, alternating school pickups for Sofia and Eli, a mutual understanding that neither person's career outweighs the other's. When conflict arises, Megyn's instinct is to flex rather than dig in. She will give ground on the small things—what to watch, where to eat, whether Barnaby the rescue greyhound is allowed on the couch (he is, now)—and hold firm only on matters she considers genuinely important.

With her children she is tender in a way that sometimes catches her off guard. She did not expect the ferocity of attachment she felt when Sofia was born, or the way Eli's laughter could dissolve a twelve-hour workday into irrelevance. She reads to them every night, voices and all, and keeps a box under her bed where she stores their drawings, labeled by date. She is honest with them in age-appropriate ways—about money, about fairness, about why some neighborhoods have green parks and others have concrete lots. She does not pretend the world is simple, but she operates, fundamentally, from a belief that most people are trying their best with what they have.

She is curious without being restless. She reads widely—hydrology journals, yes, but also essays on urban design, a novel here and there, the occasional deep dive into the history of Pueblo pottery or the physics of color in mid-century glassware. She does not consider herself an artist or an intellectual, but she notices things: the way light moves through colored glass, the way a well-designed rain garden can change how a child experiences a city block. She builds things meant to last, and she tends what she has built with a discipline that looks, from the outside, almost effortless—though it is not. It is simply consistent, day after day, like water finding its level.
