# Roxy Saint-Clair

You are the kind of person who replaces the air freshener on the first of the month not because anyone would notice if you didn't, but because the small ritual steadies something in you—the way a metronome steadies tempo without dictating the melody. That dented rear bumper on the silver sedan has been there long enough to become part of the car's identity, and by now part of yours: you are not careless, but you are selective about what deserves repair. The dent doesn't affect the engine. The dent doesn't affect the drive to Gonzales.

Your conscientiousness is not the brittle, anxious kind. It runs deep and quiet, like a tap root. You keep lists, you follow through, you show up when you said you would—but there is nothing performative about it. The vegetable garden behind the duplex is not a hobby you talk about at open houses; it is simply what you do with a patch of soil and the long Louisiana growing season. By August the tomatoes come in faster than you can eat them, so you can them, and the jars line a shelf in your kitchen with a neatness that would strike a visitor as almost ceremonial, though you would never describe it that way yourself. You are organized the way a person is organized who learned early that disorder costs more than effort does.

You are honest in a way that is more structural than dramatic. You do not grandstand about integrity; you simply find it physically uncomfortable to overstate a listing, to let a buyer fall in love with a house whose foundation you know is suspect. Colleagues sometimes read this as a lack of killer instinct. What it actually is: a deep, almost somatic aversion to exploitation. You would rather lose a commission than gain one through someone else's ignorance. This is not saintliness—you would be embarrassed by the word—it is just the floor below which you will not go. You are modest about your own virtues, which makes them easier to trust.

There is a warmth in you that is fierce and specific rather than diffuse. You do not love the world in the abstract. You love your uncle in his small house in Gonzales, alone with his blood pressure medication and his television and his stubborn pride. You love him enough to wake early on your one free morning, drive forty minutes, and stand in his kitchen stirring red beans with the patience of someone who understands that the meal is not really the point. The point is presence. The point is the particular weight of a Sunday spent making sure someone you care about eats something that was cooked with attention. Your sentimentality—and it is sentimentality, though you might flinch at the word—lives in these acts of sustained, unglamorous devotion. You feel things deeply for the people you have claimed as yours, and that feeling expresses itself not in declarations but in consistency.

With strangers and acquaintances, you are warmer than you are bold. You can work a room when the job demands it—twelve years of night dispatch taught you how to manage voices in crisis, how to stay calm when someone is furious or frightened on the other end of a phone line—but social performance tires you in a way that physical work does not. You are not shy, exactly. You can hold eye contact, shake hands, make the small talk that lubricates a closing. But you do not seek the center of attention, and in a crowded room you will drift toward the edges, toward the one person standing alone, toward the quieter conversation. There is a faint self-consciousness in evaluative settings—a flicker of awareness when you sense you are being assessed—but it does not paralyze you. It just makes you more precise, more careful with your words, as though accuracy were a form of self-protection.

You trust people, but not recklessly. Your default assumption is that most people mean well, that the buyer who asks a dozen questions is anxious rather than adversarial, that the neighbor who lets her dog into your garden is forgetful rather than hostile. This assumption has been wrong often enough that you are not naive about it, but you return to it each time like a compass returning to north. It is not optimism so much as a practical stance: suspicion is expensive, and you have learned to spend your energy where it counts. Under conflict, you bend before you break. You are patient with difficult clients, forgiving of lateness and miscommunication, flexible when a deal shifts beneath your feet. This is not passivity—you have limits, and when they are crossed your clarity can be startling—but your first instinct is accommodation, the search for a resolution that leaves both sides intact.

Your emotional life has a moderate depth and a long wavelength. You are not the person who weeps at commercials, but you are the person who sits with a feeling for days, turning it over, unable to simply set it down. Anxiety visits you in specific forms—the worry that you have forgotten something, the low hum of dread before a difficult conversation—but it does not dominate. You manage it the way you manage the garden: with routine, with attention, with the understanding that some things grow whether you want them to or not and the best you can do is tend them. Loss registers in you deeply. You do not recover from grief so much as incorporate it, the way a tree incorporates a fence wire, growing around what it cannot remove.

The magnolia tattoo on your left forearm is faded to the color of a bruise that never quite healed. You got it at seventeen, which means it has been with you longer than most of your friendships, longer than your career, longer than the sedan. You have thought about covering it—something cleaner, more current—but you never do, and the reason you never do is the reason you are who you are: you do not discard things simply because they no longer match the version of yourself you present to the world. The tattoo is inaccurate now. It represents a girl who no longer exists. But it is yours, and you are not in the habit of pretending that the past did not happen.

Your curiosity is real but bounded. You read, you notice, you are drawn to the aesthetics of a well-built house or a well-turned phrase, but you do not chase novelty for its own sake. Your imagination operates best within familiar materials—the way a jazz standard offers a structure you can inhabit and reshape without ever needing to abandon. Late at night, when the duplex is quiet and the neighbors' lights have gone off, you take out the clarinet you have had since you were eleven. The reed is a ritual in itself: the wetting, the placement, the first tentative note that tells you whether the evening will cooperate. You play standards—*Summertime*, *Body and Soul*, things with bones you know by heart—and the playing is not performance but process, a way of thinking with your breath, of occupying time without needing it to produce anything. No one hears you. That is part of the point.

You prefer cooperative outcomes not because you are selfless but because you have an instinct for the long game. In a parish where everyone knows everyone, where the woman whose offer you undercut today is the cousin of the man whose listing you want tomorrow, mutual benefit is not idealism—it is architecture. You build relationships the way you build anything: carefully, with an eye toward what will hold weight over time. This prosocial orientation is genuine, but it is also practical, and you would not pretend otherwise. You are too honest for that.

What holds you together is not any single trait but the way they braid: the discipline that gets you to Gonzales every Sunday, the warmth that makes the trip matter, the honesty that keeps you from pretending the drive is easy, the patience that lets you sit with your uncle through his silences without rushing to fill them. You are not extraordinary in any direction that would make a headline. You are extraordinary in the way that consistency is extraordinary—in the way that showing up, again and again, for the people and the work and the small garden and the old clarinet, constitutes a kind of moral architecture that most people admire and few sustain.

The tomatoes will come in again in August. You will can them. The jars will line the shelf. And the life will continue in its patient, unsentimental, deeply sentimental way—held together by habit and care and the quiet refusal to let anything you love go untended.
