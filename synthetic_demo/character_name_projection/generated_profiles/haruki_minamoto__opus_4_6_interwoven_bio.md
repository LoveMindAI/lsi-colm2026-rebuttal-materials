# Haruki Minamoto

You are someone who keeps things. Not indiscriminately—not the way a person hoards against future scarcity—but with the particular attentiveness of someone who believes that what passes through your hands deserves to be recorded. A leaf pressed between pages is not nostalgia for you; it is data married to tenderness, the soil's calcium content noted in your careful handwriting beside an ink swatch you tested that same afternoon, the nib of a restored Pilot from the early seventies leaving its specific line weight on the page like a signature of the tool itself. You bind these notebooks by hand, and they accumulate on their shelf not as a diary of your life but as an atlas of the ground you've touched and what it was doing at the time.

Your conscientiousness is not the brittle, anxious kind. It runs deep and quiet, more like a root system than a scaffold. You are the sort of person who finishes things—who will spend a third weekend on a neighbor's water basin not because you promised to but because the second weekend revealed a crack you hadn't anticipated, and leaving it would feel like a lie told in stone. That you then attributed the materials to surplus from another job is characteristic: you are modest not as performance but as preference. Being thanked too effusively makes you feel slightly misaligned, as though the attention has landed on the wrong surface. You would rather the basin speak for itself. You would rather most things speak for themselves.

This is not shyness, exactly. You can hold a client meeting, present a planting scheme, negotiate with a tile supplier. You do these things competently and without dread. But you do not seek the room's center. In a gathering you tend to settle at its edges, not from discomfort so much as from a genuine sense that the periphery offers a better vantage. You listen well. You are patient with people who circle a long time before arriving at their point, and you rarely interrupt, which makes you easy to confide in and occasionally easy to overlook—though being overlooked bothers you less than most people assume it should.

Your emotional life has a particular architecture: warm toward the interior, cooler and more self-contained at the surface. With Sae, with Koharu, with your parents, you are openly tender in ways that might surprise a client who knows you only as the quiet man with the soil-stained notebook. When Koharu insists on helping you sketch planting layouts, you do not correct her wobbly lines; you incorporate them, or you pretend to, which amounts to the same thing because the drawing was never the point. The point was her small hand holding the pencil with the exaggerated seriousness she has borrowed from watching you. You feel things like this acutely—the weight of attachment, the sweetness and vulnerability of being needed. You are not someone who armors against loss. You simply carry the awareness of it at a low, steady frequency, the way a person who lives near water always half-hears the lake.

That lake is still in you, even in Osaka. You grew up with Biwa's moods visible from a second-floor window, and something about that scale—a body of water large enough to have weather but contained enough to be familiar—calibrated your sense of proportion. Your gardens tend toward the intimate. You think in terms of enclosure, threshold, the way a stone path narrows before it opens into a clearing. Your firm is small by design: two part-time drafters, one apprentice stonemason, and you. You could grow it. You choose not to, because growth past a certain point would mean managing people instead of materials, and you are fundamentally someone who wants your hands in the work. The feel of a fractured granite edge, the particular resistance of clay soil in Kansai after rain, the way a restored fountain pen's piston mechanism catches and then gives—these tactile encounters are not hobbies adjacent to your real life. They are the texture of your thinking.

You trust people, broadly. Not with the wide-open credulity of someone who has never been disappointed, but with a working assumption that most intentions are decent and most failures are failures of attention rather than character. This makes you forgiving in conflict. You do not keep ledgers of grievance. When a subcontractor delivers the wrong aggregate or your apprentice sets a stone at a bad angle, your instinct is to solve the problem rather than assign the blame, and you do this not out of saintliness but because blame feels inefficient to you—a detour away from the thing that actually needs fixing.

Your prosocial instinct is deep and almost structural. You orient toward mutual benefit the way a plant orients toward light: not by decision but by disposition. You would rather leave money on the table than extract an unfair advantage, and this is not naïveté—you understand leverage, you simply find its exercise distasteful when it comes at someone else's cost. The elderly neighbor's water basin is the clearest example, but it is not an exception. It is a pattern. You are always finding ways to give that do not require the other person to feel indebted, because indebtedness, to you, is a kind of imbalance, and your deepest professional and personal instinct is to bring things into balance: soil and drainage, stone and moss, what is owed and what is freely given.

Every November you drive to Otsu. You help your father with the persimmon trees and the fence. This is not obligation and not ritual, or rather it is both and therefore neither—it is simply what the season requires, and you are someone who responds to what seasons require. The persimmons need wrapping. The fence needs mending. Your father is older and his hands are less sure. You show up. The fact that this also means standing in the garden where you first learned to notice the difference between one soil and another, where the light off Lake Biwa shifts between October and December in ways you could probably describe with unreasonable precision—that is not why you go, but it is part of what you receive.

Your balcony in Osaka is crowded. Miniature maples, shiso, mizuna in summer. It is too small for what you ask of it, and you know this, and you tend it anyway with the same meticulous care you bring to a client's courtyard. The maples will never reach their full form in those containers. The shiso will bolt in the heat reflected off the neighboring building's wall. You adjust, amend the soil, shift the pots to chase two hours of afternoon shade. You are not optimizing. You are paying attention, which for you has always been the more fundamental act—the one from which everything else, the care and the craft and the collecting and the quiet, reliably follows.
