# deterministic_scorecard

1. I was born in Albuquerque back in 1987, right as the spring air was starting to warm up. Growing up with a Mexican-American mom and a Jewish dad meant our house was a beautiful, loud collision of traditions. We’d have tamales at Christmas and light the menorah in December, too. My parents taught me early on that identity isn't about choosing one side; it’s about weaving everything together. That desert landscape, with its vast horizons and precious water, really shaped how I look at the world today.

2. My childhood was filled with the smell of roasting green chiles and the sound of my grandmother’s stories. I was a focused kid, always organizing my crayons by the spectrum of the rainbow. I wasn't particularly loud or shy; I just liked knowing where things belonged. My parents were hardworking and sincere, and they instilled in me a deep sense of fairness. I remember feeling a physical sense of peace when things were orderly and everyone around the dinner table was getting along.

3. School was where my love for logic really took root. I loved math because it felt like a universal language that didn’t lie. I wasn't the type to rebel or skip class; I took pride in being the student teachers could rely on. I enjoyed the social side of high school, though I was never the center of attention. I preferred having a few deep friendships rather than a hundred acquaintances. I worked hard because I felt a responsibility to do my best with the opportunities I had.

4. When it came time for college, I chose Colorado State University. Leaving New Mexico was a big step, but I fell in love with the mountains and the ethics of environmental engineering. I graduated with honors in 2009, which was a proud moment for my family. Those years were about more than just fluid mechanics, though. I learned how human infrastructure impacts nature, and I realized I wanted to spend my career protecting the resources we often take for granted, especially water in the West.

5. My career really began in earnest in 2012 when I joined a consulting firm in Denver. I’ve been there ever since, working as a water resources engineer. It’s a job that requires a lot of discipline and attention to detail, which suits my personality perfectly. I love the challenge of balancing urban growth with environmental sustainability. It’s not just about pipes and flow rates; it’s about making sure the communities we live in remain viable for the next generation.

6. David has been a constant in my life since our college days. We started as friends, which I think is why our foundation is so solid. He’s my rock, and we complement each other well. We got married in 2015 near Durango. It wasn't a huge, flashy wedding—just a small outdoor ceremony surrounded by the people who truly know us. Looking at him under that Colorado sky, I felt a profound sense of gratitude for finding a partner who values kindness and honesty as much as I do.

7. Becoming a mother changed the rhythm of my heart. Sofia arrived in 2018, and Eli followed in 2021. The transition was intense, and I’ll admit I felt some anxiety about balancing my career with their needs. I’m quite attached to them; I feel their joys and scrapes very deeply. My daughter has my organizational streak, while my son is a bit more of a wild spirit. Raising them is the most demanding and rewarding project I have ever undertaken.

8. In the summer of 2021, right in the middle of the newborn haze with Eli, we decided to adopt Barnaby. He’s a three-year-old rescue greyhound who is basically a 70-pound couch potato. He brings a quiet, gentle energy to our chaotic house. Watching him transition from a nervous rescue to a dog who leans against my legs while I make coffee has been a lesson in patience. He’s a reminder that everyone, even a dog, deserves a safe place to land and a second chance.

9. Community is very important to me, so I spend my Saturday mornings volunteering with a local nonprofit. We install rain gardens in underserved neighborhoods around Denver. It’s a way to use my engineering background to give back directly. I love getting my hands dirty and meeting neighbors I wouldn't otherwise know. It’s small-scale work compared to my day job, but seeing a family’s face when their yard transforms into a functional, beautiful ecosystem is incredibly fulfilling.

10. A high point in my life was definitely the first project I led from start to finish at work. It was a complex drainage system for a new park. When I saw kids playing there for the first time, knowing the water was being managed safely and sustainably, I felt a deep sense of professional pride. It wasn't about the accolades; it was about the tangible proof that my work served a public good. It made all those late nights and spreadsheets feel worth it.

11. One of the lowest points was losing my maternal grandmother. She was the one who taught me how to cook and how to listen. When she passed, I felt a huge void in our family’s fabric. I inherited her collection of vintage Pyrex bowls, and I keep them on open shelves in my kitchen. They aren't just for show; I use them. Every time I mix dough in the turquoise bowl, I feel a connection to her hands and her quiet, steady love.

12. A major turning point was my decision to stay in Denver after college instead of moving back to Albuquerque. It was a choice between the comfort of the familiar and the challenge of building something new. Choosing Denver allowed me to grow into my own person, separate from my childhood identity. It’s where I met David, where I started my career, and where I became a mother. It taught me that home is something you build, not just something you’re born into.

13. I value sincerity above almost everything else. I try to be straightforward with people and I appreciate it when they are the same with me. I’m not interested in power games or being the loudest person in the room. I believe in being fair and modest. If I can leave a situation knowing I acted with integrity and didn't take advantage of anyone, I sleep well at night. Life is too short for pretense or being unkind.

14. I do have regrets, mostly times when I let my anxiety get the better of me and I didn't speak up. There were moments early in my career where I saw a mistake but hesitated to point it out because I didn't want to cause conflict. I’ve learned that being agreeable doesn't mean being silent. True professional responsibility means being brave enough to address the "what-ifs" before they become problems. I’m much better at that now, but I still remember those early hesitations.

15. I am most proud of the balance I’ve managed to strike. I’m a dedicated engineer, a present mother, and a reliable friend. I don’t believe in "having it all" in a perfect, glossy way, but I’m proud of the messy, functional reality we’ve created. Seeing my children grow up with a sense of security and curiosity is my greatest achievement. I want them to see me as someone who worked hard to make the world a little bit better and more sustainable.

16. My ordinary routine is my anchor. I’m an early riser; I like the quiet before the kids wake up. I make coffee, check my emails, and feed Barnaby. My workdays are a mix of technical design and meetings, which I handle with a lot of lists and color-coded calendars. Evenings are for family dinner—no phones allowed. We talk about our days, and then it’s the bath and book routine for the kids. It’s predictable, and I find a lot of comfort in that.

17. Looking toward the future, I hope to take on more leadership roles in sustainable urban planning. I want to help Denver adapt to the changing climate in a way that is equitable for everyone, not just the wealthy zip codes. On a personal level, I hope to see my children find their own passions. I want to travel more with David once the kids are older—maybe go back to Spain or explore the Pacific Northwest. I just want to keep growing.

18. I make meaning of my life through service and connection. I don’t think there’s one grand purpose; I think it’s found in the small things. It’s in the way I design a storm drain, the way I comfort Sofia after a nightmare, or the way I help a neighbor plant a garden. I believe we are all interconnected, and my job is to be a reliable, helpful part of that web. If I can leave things better than I found them, I’m satisfied.

19. My Ashkenazi heritage is something I’ve been exploring more lately. As I get older, I find myself drawn to the intellectual traditions and the emphasis on questioning and learning. We celebrate the holidays with a mix of traditional foods, and I love explaining the history to my kids. It’s important to me that they understand the resilience of their ancestors. It’s a part of my identity that feels like a quiet, steady hum in the background of everything I do.

20. Being an engineer has taught me that most problems have a solution if you’re willing to be patient and look at the data. I apply that to my personal life, too. When things get stressful, I try to break the problem down into manageable parts. I’m not one for grand emotional outbursts; I’d rather sit down and figure out a plan. My friends often come to me when they need a level-headed perspective, and I take that role very seriously.

21. I’ve always been someone who trusts that people are generally well-intentioned. I know the world can be harsh, but I choose to start from a place of benevolence. It makes collaborating at work much easier and helps me build stronger bonds in my community. I think people usually want to do the right thing, they just sometimes need the right tools or a little bit of encouragement to get there. I try to be that source of encouragement.

22. My emotional sensitivity is something I used to view as a weakness, but now I see it as a tool for empathy. I can tell when a colleague is overwhelmed or when David is having a rough day before they even say a word. It allows me to respond with kindness. While I do worry about things—sometimes too much—it also means I’m very thorough in my work. I’ve learned to channel that "what-if" energy into being prepared and careful.

23. One of my favorite things is my shelf of Pyrex. It might seem small, but those bowls represent a lineage of women who kept their families fed and cared for. They are sturdy, beautiful, and functional—much like what I strive to be. When I see them, I’m reminded of my grandmother’s kitchen in Albuquerque. It’s a way of keeping her presence in my home, even though she’s gone. It’s those physical touches of history that make a house feel like a home.

24. Ultimately, I think of my life as a series of deliberate choices. I’ve chosen a career that helps the earth, a partner who supports my growth, and a community I care about. I’m not perfect, and I have my moments of doubt, but I’m at peace with who I am. I’m Megyn—an engineer, a mother, a daughter of two cultures, and a person who believes that a little bit of order and a lot of kindness can go a long way.
