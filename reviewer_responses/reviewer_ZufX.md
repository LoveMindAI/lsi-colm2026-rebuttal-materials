# What PARSEL Can and Cannot Settle

Thank you for focusing on the central interpretive question: whether the result reflects learned personality-language structure or only a trait-portrayal schema. We cannot fully settle that question from the present conversation data alone. PARSEL contains psychometrics, dyadic conversations, and partner impressions, but not matched human-authored life-story interviews. Without human life stories collected under the same elicitation format, we cannot claim that generated life-story dynamics reproduce human life-story dynamics. We sought access to two such datasets during the initial experimental phase but could not secure matched access in time; if accepted, we will state this as a central follow-up rather than an omitted analysis.

**Trait-schema concern.** To further test the trait-schema account, we re-examined the existing emotional-reactivity result. For each 24-turn generated life story, we computed average emotional valence and within-story SD of section-level emotional valence, then tested whether source traits predict variability while controlling for average valence. Emotionality remains predictive, so the effect is not simply that "emotional people write more emotional prose." The strongest predictor is HEXACO Sentimentality, not Anxiety or Fearfulness. PPTS affective-responsiveness deficits and Trust Benevolence point to the same affective-warmth channel. If accepted, this will provide convergent evidence for the existing emotional-reactivity result.

**Evaluator dependence.** To test whether the result depends on one evaluator, we re-coded emotional valence with four independent LLM coders; the Sentimentality-to-valence-variability effect replicated for all eight generator/profile lanes, with mean-controlled partial correlations of roughly `r = 0.42-0.50`. It also does not appear to be default generator behaviour: unconditioned 24-turn narratives have narrower emotional-valence variability (`0.070-0.088`) than conditioned lanes (`0.102-0.131`). A coarse schema for "portraying an emotional person" would most naturally predict shifts in average tone; it does not naturally predict mean-independent, facet-specific, cross-instrument, cross-generator, and cross-coder modulation. If accepted, we will add these as bounded robustness checks for the existing reactivity analysis.

We will still avoid overclaiming the source. Our result fits recent context-sensitive accounts of LLM personality expression better than a simple "Assistant with a trait-schema skill" account, but we cannot identify the precise training-data source. We will frame the claim as structured, context-sensitive psychometric encoding in generated life-story behaviour, not proof that the model has learned the full human personality-language relationship.

**Figure 3 and metric matching.** You are correct that the submitted Figure 3 mixed Pearson `r` for generated life-story recovery with Spearman rho for real-conversation scoring. We recomputed the comparison using Pearson correlations for both. Short real PARSEL conversations contain modest recoverable HEXACO signal, `r = 0.179-0.221` depending on scorer ensemble. By contrast, 24-turn generated life stories recover HEXACO/Beyond-HEXACO at `0.787/0.619` for psychometric-only profiles and `0.761/0.613` for psychometric-plus-biographical profiles. The gap is real: round-trip recovery supports psychometric information surviving transformation into generated life-story form, but does not by itself show faithful short-conversation behaviour. If accepted, we will remove the current Figure 3 from the main text and replace it with text explaining this comparison and its limits.

**Conversation denominators.** Participants' own HEXACO scores predict coded conversational behaviour only modestly: mean `|r| = 0.094`, with a featurewise best-trait reference of `0.181`. This contextualises the generated-story-to-real-conversation same-feature correspondence of `r = 0.147-0.180`, which is statistically significant but modest. PARSEL partner-perceived HEXACO also correlates with self-report at mean `r = 0.157`, reinforcing that the available conversations contain limited recoverable trait signal.

**Format mismatch.** Finally, we will distinguish two questions. The submitted cross-context analysis is a level correspondence; the narrative-reactivity analysis is a variability question. Estimating variability from roughly four short conversations per participant is mechanically noisy, whereas the life-story result is estimated from 24 sections. Wright et al.'s dose-response evidence points the same way: they report `r = 0.44-0.48` from much richer self-narration, while PARSEL has about 12.7 minutes and 1,110 target-speaker words per participant in a less introspective format. If accepted, we will frame the present conversation result as modest behavioural triangulation, not as a strong simulation claim.
